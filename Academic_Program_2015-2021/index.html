A Co 3-238th Non-Rated Aircrew Member Training Program

2015-2021

During initiation the project charter was developed and signed off on annually by the State Army Aviation Officer (SAAO) in the form of a series of memorandums outlining his intentions for the year. These documents were then provided to me and the standardization instructor pilot for our company to begin the planning phase with the various company level stakeholders.

The annual scope was generally easy to define and rarely changed for aircrew academics. There are specific subject areas that each aircrew member must be trained on initially, and refreshed on annually with an exam documented annually to remain current on their assigned aircraft. These requirements were continuously monitored and controlled throughout the training cycle so that if any requirements ever changed they would be updated and implemented immediately across all levels.

Scheduling training for an Army National Guard force can provide similar challenges to that of a dispersed workforce. Personnel with the additional requirements that come with a flight slot generally commit themselves to additional periods of duty during the week to meet their flight requirements. It is during this time that our instructor team were ever vigilant in continuously monitoring our members schedules so that we could complete our required additional training and evaluations with them.

Costs were predetermined the year prior to each training year by the SAAO and our upper echelon personnel (PMO). I would assist in developing the planning and cost estimates and they would later return an approved cost worksheet and training execution matrix. These costs could be adjusted later in the year if our justification request was approved on a case by case basis.

Planning and identifying risk is an ongoing and important step that is never overlooked in aviation. It must be monitored and controlled throughout every step of the process. Therefore, it was an integral piece in my program as well. I integrated risk planning and management by ensuring that there was adequate time for safety briefings to be conducted prior to flights and that non-rated air crew members were actively participating in them. I also utilized real world experiences when providing training scenarios to drive the importance of risk management home to newer aircrew members.

My goal was to develop and implement a program that would ensure that all non-rated aircrew members received training that consistently provided them with training that not only met the standards set forth by the applicable regulations but also solidified the information in a way that they would be able to act from muscle memory under pressure if the need ever arose.

To do this I relied on a variation of methods and empowered the rest of my instructor team to engage our crew members in the same way. Virtual training was broken down into subsections by month, so we would develop flight scenarios that focused on malfunctions that could arise in relation to the same subsections that our crew members saw during their OAATS training that month. We would then take it a step further and often ask them to correlate those scenarios into other required tasks such as emergency egress and marshaling passengers to a designated area. The are scenarios that they may encounter in a real life situation but could easily forget if not given the opportunity to break down what is happening in a controlled environment over and over again first.

Our artifacts were originally stored in Sharepoint which more recently underwent a migration to MS Teams. During our personnel's monthly or annual duty period we would gather lessons learned both formally and informally and file them for collection and consolidation at the end of the year for our SAAO and PMO's review and sign off.
